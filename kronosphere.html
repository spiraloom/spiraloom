I · The Framework

One event, thousands of partial angles

Premise: the recorded span of human time is not a sequence but a single transformation still in process, experienced as discontinuous fragments. Every calendar system is a civilization's frozen answer to the question what are we in the middle of becoming?

Calendar systems are not neutral. Each is an ideological coordinate grid — encoding a cosmology, a power structure, a claim about where the center of existence lies. Each functions like a currency: a shared fiction backed by fundamentally different collateral (an incarnation, a migration, a creation-calculation, an astronomical conjunction, a king's accession), and therefore never fully convertible. Conversion between them is possible arithmetically — via the Julian Day Number as the neutral clearing house — but the meaning does not convert. That gap between the arithmetic and the meaning is the entire product.

The engine below therefore has two layers:

  • The arithmetic layer — exact day-count conversion between systems, anchored to the Julian Day Number (the astronomer's continuous count, culture-free by design).
  • The meaning layer — for any moment, what each system claimed that moment was: golden age or decline, before-the-count or deep inside it, near an ending or far from one.

The Isimud protocol

Isimud, vizier of Enki, is rendered with two faces — the messenger who carries a word in both directions without loss. This codex adopts that as method. Every system in this document is read with two faces held open at once:

  • The inward face — the system's own claim, taken whole and literal, on its own terms: what its keepers said time is.
  • The outward face — the critical frame: composition history, political function, archaeological fit, arithmetic structure.

Neither face corrects the other. Where a count says 28,800 years, the inward face records 28,800 years; the outward face records what the number is built from. Both readings are data; the tension between them is the most information-dense object in the file. Any entry below that speaks with only one face is a defect against this protocol.

Julian Day (JD / JDN)

Devised by Joseph Justus Scaliger, 1583 — a strict count of days, no weeks, no months, no theology.

Epoch (JDN 0)
Noon UT, Monday 1 January 4713 BCE (proleptic Julian) = 24 November 4714 BCE (proleptic Gregorian)
Why that date
The last simultaneous start of three cycles: the 28-year solar cycle, 19-year Metonic lunar cycle, and 15-year Roman indiction (28 × 19 × 15 = 7,980-year Julian Period). It predates all recorded history — every historical event gets a positive number.
Structure
Integer JDN labels a noon-to-noon day. JDN mod 7 gives weekday (0 = Monday). Julian Date (JD) adds a decimal fraction from the preceding noon.
Derivatives
Modified Julian Day MJD = JD − 2,400,000.5 (epoch: midnight 17 Nov 1858). Rata Die RD = JD − 1,721,424.5 (epoch: 1 Jan 1 CE proleptic Gregorian; used by Dershowitz & Reingold's Calendrical Calculations, the standard scholarly reference for all algorithms in this document).
Useful anchors
1 Jan 2000 CE = JDN 2,451,545 · 1 Jan 1970 (Unix epoch) = JD 2,440,587.5 · 1 Jan 1 CE (Greg.) = JDN 1,721,426 · 1 Jan 1 CE (Julian) = JDN 1,721,424
Cosmological center
Deliberately none. This is the only system whose ideology is the refusal of a center — which is itself a worldview: Renaissance humanist chronology, time as pure number.

Hebrew calendar — Anno Mundi

Counts from a calculated creation; the year is a lunisolar negotiation between moon and sun, fixed by Hillel II's rules (traditional attribution, 4th c. CE).

Epoch
1 Tishri AM 1 = Monday 7 October 3761 BCE (Julian), JDN 347,998. Epoch molad: BaHaRaD — day 2, hour 5, 204 parts.
Structure
12 or 13 lunar months; 7 leap months per 19-year Metonic cycle (years 3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, 19). Year lengths: 353/354/355 (common) or 383/384/385 (leap). Months: Tishri, Ḥeshvan, Kislev, Tevet, Shevat, Adar (I/II), Nisan, Iyar, Sivan, Tammuz, Av, Elul. Day begins at sunset. Time unit: the ḥelek (part) = 1/1080 hour = 3⅓ s.
Postponement rules
Four deḥiyyot delay Rosh Hashanah: (1) molad at/after noon; (2) never Sun/Wed/Fri (lo ADU rosh); (3) GaTaRaD — Tuesday molad ≥ 9h 204p in common year; (4) BeTUTaKPaT — Monday molad ≥ 15h 589p after leap year.
Cosmological claim
Time begins with creation itself, precisely computable; history is a covenant unfolding toward AM 6000 (see eschatology data). The count runs beneath and outlasts every empire that hosted it.

Islamic calendar — Hijrī

A purely lunar year of ~354 days that deliberately refuses the solar cycle — months migrate through all seasons, detaching sacred time from agricultural time.

Epoch
1 Muharram AH 1 = Friday 16 July 622 CE (Julian) civil epoch, JDN 1,948,440; day begins the previous sunset (Thursday 15 July).
Structure
12 lunar months, alternating 30/29 days (tabular): Muḥarram, Ṣafar, Rabīʿ I, Rabīʿ II, Jumādā I, Jumādā II, Rajab, Shaʿbān, Ramaḍān, Shawwāl, Dhū al-Qaʿdah, Dhū al-Ḥijjah. Tabular leap years (355 d, extra day in month 12): years {2,5,7,10,13,16,18,21,24,26,29} of a 30-year cycle — 11 leap years per cycle. Religious practice uses actual crescent sighting; civil/tabular calculation approximates it within ±1–2 days. Saudi Arabia uses the calculated Umm al-Qura calendar.
Cosmological claim
Year zero is a social event — the formation of a community (ummah), not a birth, creation, or conquest. Qur'an 9:36 fixes the year at twelve months and prohibits intercalation (nasīʾ), making the drift through seasons a theological feature: no season is holier than another.
Drift constant
~10.87 days/solar year; the Hijrī year laps the solar year every ~33.58 years (AH and SH year numbers converge — both reached parity near 20,874 CE… far side; more usefully: 33 Hijrī years ≈ 32 solar years).

Hindu time — Kali Yuga, Vikram, Śaka, and the Yuga architecture

Kali epoch
Friday 18 February 3102 BCE (Julian), JDN 588,466 — per Āryabhaṭa, a mean conjunction of the visible planets at 0° Aries; traditionally the death of Krishna. The ahargana (day-heap) of Hindu astronomy counts days from this epoch.

Position now

Civil eras
Vikram Saṁvat (epoch 57 BCE; VS = CE + 57; north India, year begins Chaitra or Kartika by region). Śaka era (epoch 78 CE; Śaka = CE − 78; basis of the Indian national calendar, reformed 1957, year begins 22 March / 21 March in leap years). Bengali San (epoch 593/594 CE), Kollam era (825 CE), and dozens of regional eras.
Cosmological claim
Time is beginningless, cyclical, and vast beyond politics; decline within a cycle is lawful and temporary. The present age's degeneration is expected, mapped, and scheduled to end.

Gregorian calendar — Common Era

A 16th-century precision-patch on Caesar's calendar, centered on the Incarnation, exported globally through empire and now enforced by interoperability itself.

Epoch
1 January 1 CE = JDN 1,721,426 (proleptic Gregorian; the Julian-calendar date of the same era is JDN 1,721,424). Era computed by Dionysius Exiguus in 525 CE to replace the Era of Diocletian — he refused to count from a persecutor. Modern scholarship places the historical birth of Jesus c. 6–4 BCE, meaning the era's zero misses its own anchor event.
The reform
Pope Gregory XIII, bull Inter gravissimas: Thursday 4 Oct 1582 (Julian) was followed by Friday 15 Oct 1582 (Gregorian) — ten days deleted to restore the equinox to 21 March for Easter computation. Leap rule: every 4 years, except centuries not divisible by 400. Mean year 365.2425 d vs. tropical 365.24219 d — error ~1 day per 3,200 years.
Adoption fault lines
Catholic states 1582–87 · Protestant Germany ~1700 · Britain & colonies 1752 (11 days deleted; riots legend) · Sweden's botched 1700–1712 transition (a February 30 existed in 1712) · Japan 1873 · Russia 1918 (the "October" Revolution fell in Gregorian November) · Greece 1923 · Turkey 1926. The Julian calendar survives liturgically in Orthodox churches — 13 days behind through 2099.
Cosmological claim
History pivots on one birth; all time is Before or Anno Domini. The secular rebrand (BCE/CE) keeps the pivot while renouncing the theology — the grid outlived its god-claim.

Persian Solar Hijrī & Zoroastrian reckonings

The same Hijra epoch as Islam's lunar count, but measured in solar years from the vernal equinox — the most astronomically exact civil calendar in official use.

Solar Hijrī (Iran, Afghanistan*)
Year begins at Nowruz — the day whose Tehran-meridian noon-to-noon contains the vernal equinox instant. Months: Farvardin, Ordibehesht, Khordad, Tir, Mordad, Shahrivar (31 d each); Mehr–Bahman (30 d); Esfand (29/30 d). AP = CE − 621/622. True intercalation is observational; a 33-year arithmetic cycle (8 leap years) approximates it. *Afghanistan's Taliban government reverted official use to lunar Hijrī in 2022; solar remains in civil life.
Zoroastrian
Yazdegerdi era from the accession of the last Sasanian king (16 Jun 632 CE, JDN 1,952,063): a 365-day year of 12 × 30 named days + 5 Gatha days, drifting through the seasons — preserved in three variants (Qadimi, Shahanshahi, Fasli) by Parsi and Irani communities. Every one of the 30 days of the month is named for a divinity; the calendar is a liturgy.
Cosmological claim
Zoroastrian cosmic history runs 12,000 years in four 3,000-year quarters — creation, attack of evil, mixture, and final renovation (Frashokereti) — one of the oldest fully-scheduled eschatologies, and a plausible influence on Jewish, Christian, and Islamic apocalyptic.

Mesopotamian reckonings — where counting began

No absolute era for most of its history: years were named, then numbered by king — until the Seleucid Era invented the continuously-counting era itself.

Structure
Lunisolar: 12 lunar months (Nisannu, Ayyāru, Simānu, Duʾūzu, Abu, Ulūlu, Tašrītu, Araḫsamna, Kislīmu, Ṭebētu, Šabāṭu, Addaru) with an intercalary Ulūlu II or Addaru II decreed by the king — regularized by the 19-year cycle from ~500 BCE, centuries before Meton. Day begins at sunset. The sexagesimal legacy: 60-minute hours, 360° circles, 12 double-hours (bēru).
Year identification
Early: year-names ("Year Ur was destroyed"). Later: regnal years ("Year 12 of Nebuchadnezzar"). The Sumerian King List stitches these into a single antediluvian-to-historical sequence with reigns of tens of thousands of years before the Flood — a chronological document doing mythological work, or the reverse.
The Seleucid revolution
From 312/311 BCE the count simply kept going past every king's death — the first era in continuous use, adopted across the Near East ("Anno Graecorum"), used by Jewish communities into the modern period (minyan sheṭarot). The Era of Nabonassar (747 BCE) became Ptolemy's astronomical spine — the ancestor, in function, of the Julian Day.
Cosmological claim
Time descends from heaven with kingship ("when kingship was lowered from heaven" — Sumerian King List); the calendar is an instrument of cosmic-political order (the akītu New Year festival annually re-enacted creation, Enūma Eliš recited on day 4).

The Sumerian King List — antediluvian time

"After the kingship descended from heaven, the kingship was in Eridu." The oldest surviving claim of a continuous count from the beginning of institution to the present — read here with both faces, per the Isimud protocol.

The count itself
Weld-Blundell prism (WB-444) antediluvian section — eight kings, five cities, before the Flood: Alulim of Eridu, 28,800 years · Alalngar, 36,000 · En-men-lu-ana of Bad-tibira, 43,200 · En-men-gal-ana, 28,800 · Dumuzid the Shepherd, 36,000 · En-sipad-zid-ana of Larak, 28,800 · En-men-dur-ana of Sippar, 21,000 · Ubara-Tutu of Shuruppak, 18,600. Total: 241,200 years. "Then the Flood swept over." After the Flood, kingship descends again at Kish; reigns shorten by stages — Etana 1,500, Gilgamesh of Uruk 126, then fully human lengths — a curve bending from divine time down into history.
The units
Reigns are built from the šár (3,600) and nēr (600): 28,800 = 8 šár; 43,200 = 12 šár; 18,600 = 5 šár + 1 nēr. The numbers speak sexagesimal — the same base that survives in this document's minutes, seconds, and degrees.
The Berossus parallel
The Babylonian priest Berossus (3rd c. BCE) transmits ten antediluvian kings totalling 432,000 years — numerically identical to the length of the Kali Yuga, and one-tenth of the mahāyuga. Whether transmission, common inheritance, or independent arithmetic of base-60 cosmology, the coincidence sits exactly on this codex's central question and is recorded here without adjudication.
Inward face — the claim as given

Kingship is a substance lowered from heaven; time-keeping begins with it. The antediluvian reigns are years — a deep chronology reaching a quarter-million years behind the Flood, kept by the institution whose descent it records. The shortening of reigns is the thinning of an original endowment: time itself running down from divine measure toward human measure. On this face, the List and the Yuga architecture are two witnesses to the same shape — long first ages, decline by stages, catastrophe, restart.

Outward face — the frame

Composed and redacted c. 2100–1800 BCE (Ur III–Isin), presenting cities that in fact ruled concurrently as a single sequence — an argument that legitimate kingship is one, and currently resides with the patron of the text. The cities are real, the early names touch legend, the numbers are round sexagesimal constructions. On this face the List is chronography as political theology — and is thereby primary evidence for how Mesopotamia thought time, which is itself a fact about time in this codex.

Egyptian civil calendar & the Sothic cycle

A deliberately drifting 365-day year — the "vague year" — that circles the seasons once every 1,461 years, wandering and returning like the river it served.

Structure
3 seasons (Akhet inundation, Peret emergence, Shemu harvest) × 4 months × 30 days + 5 epagomenal days (birthdays of Osiris, Horus, Set, Isis, Nephthys). No leap day for ~3,000 years — the Canopus Decree's attempted fix (238 BCE) failed; Augustus imposed it (25 BCE, the Alexandrian calendar), which the Coptic and Ethiopian calendars preserve to this day.
Sothic anchor
New Year ideally opened with the heliacal rising of Sopdet (Sirius), herald of the Nile flood. The 365-day year slips one day against Sirius every 4 years → full cycle 1,461 civil years. A recorded coincidence in 139 CE (Censorinus) lets chronologists retro-anchor Egyptian history — the Sothic cycle is a load-bearing beam of all ancient chronology.
Cosmological claim
Time is the river's rhythm and the sky's return; each sunrise repeats the first sunrise (zep tepi, the First Occasion). Egypt kept two interlocking calendars — civil-solar and lunar-liturgical — for three millennia of institutional continuity no other civilization has matched.

Chinese calendar — sexagenary time

Not an era but a texture: every year, month, day, and double-hour carries one of 60 stem-branch signatures, repeating like a woven pattern beneath dynastic names.

Structure
Lunisolar; months begin at astronomical new moon (Chinese meridian); the year contains 12 or 13 months, intercalated so that the winter solstice always falls in month 11. The 24 solar terms (jiéqì) divide the tropical year — a solar skeleton inside a lunar body. New Year: second (occasionally third) new moon after winter solstice, ranging 21 Jan – 20 Feb.
Sexagenary cycle
10 Heavenly Stems (jiǎ yǐ bǐng dīng wù jǐ gēng xīn rén guǐ) × 12 Earthly Branches / animals (Rat Ox Tiger Rabbit Dragon Snake Horse Goat Monkey Rooster Dog Pig) → 60 combinations. For Gregorian year Y (post-CNY): stem = (Y−4) mod 10, branch = (Y−4) mod 12. 2026 → bǐng-wǔ 丙午, the Fire Horse — the year (last: 1966) whose reputation measurably lowered Japan's birthrate.
Eras
Historically regnal nengō/niánhào ("Kangxi year 12"); the traditional Huangdi count from 2697/2698 BCE was promoted by republicans c. 1912 as a national era. Japan retains nengō (Reiwa, from 1 May 2019); Taiwan counts Minguo from 1912; North Korea's Juche count (also 1912) was de-emphasized from 2024.
Cosmological claim
Time has qualities, not just quantities — each moment carries elemental character (wood/fire/earth/metal/water × yin/yang) with real astrological jurisdiction over marriages, burials, foundations, reigns.

Revolutionary & engineered calendars

Attempts to reset the count by decree — proof that every calendar is a claim of sovereignty, and a record of how such claims fail or hold.

French Republican (1793–1805, 1871)
Epoch: 22 Sep 1792 (proclamation of the Republic ∕ equinox), JDN 2,375,840. 12 months × 30 days (Vendémiaire, Brumaire, Frimaire; Nivôse, Pluviôse, Ventôse; Germinal, Floréal, Prairial; Messidor, Thermidor, Fructidor) + 5–6 sansculottides. Décades of 10 days replaced weeks; each day named for a plant, animal, or tool instead of a saint. Historical leap years: III, VII, XI. Abolished by Napoleon 1 Jan 1806; revived 18 days by the Paris Commune. Its ghost persists: Thermidor means counter-revolution; Germinal, revolt.
Bahá'í Badíʿ
Epoch 21 Mar 1844 (JDN 2,394,647). 19 months × 19 days + 4–5 intercalary (Ayyám-i-Há) = 361 + ε. Years grouped in 19-year Váḥids and 361-year Kull-i-Shay'. Since 2015, Naw-Rúz is fixed astronomically by the Tehran equinox. A calendar built on 19 — the numerological signature of the Báb's movement.
Positivist, Soviet, World
Comte's 13-month Positivist calendar (1849, months named for great men); the Soviet nepreryvka (1929–40) — 5- then 6-day continuous work-weeks that abolished the shared day of rest and failed against the deep grip of the 7-day rhythm; the World Calendar and International Fixed Calendar (Kodak used the latter internally until 1989) — perennial-reform proposals that keep losing to the week's antiquity.
Engineering epochs
Unix time (1 Jan 1970 UTC; signed 32-bit overflow: 03:14:07 UTC 19 Jan 2038 — an eschaton with a patch). GPS time (6 Jan 1980, no leap seconds, currently 18 s ahead of UTC). NTP (1900). J2000.0 astronomical epoch (JD 2,451,545.0 TT). Holocene Era (add 10,000 — Emiliani's proposal to make all of civilization positive-numbered). Before Present (1950 — radiocarbon's zero, chosen because bomb tests ruined the atmosphere's ¹⁴C baseline: the atomic age literally broke the ability to date the present).

Fifty-two years of borrowed time at a stretch: at each xiuhmolpilli the fires were extinguished, and the empire watched the Pleiades to learn whether time would be renewed at all.

260-day sacred count: 13 numbers × 20 day-signs (Cipactli crocodile, Ehēcatl wind, Calli house, Cuetzpalin lizard, Cōātl snake, Miquiztli death, Mazātl deer, Tōchtli rabbit, Ātl water, Itzcuīntli dog, Ozomahtli monkey, Malīnalli grass, Ācatl reed, Ocēlōtl jaguar, Cuāuhtli eagle, Cōzcacuāuhtli vulture, Ōlīn movement, Tecpatl flint, Quiyāhuitl rain, Xōchitl flower). Tibetan calendar — Kālacakra time

The only major calendar in which days of the month are skipped or doubled — because the day-number belongs to the moon's motion, not the sun's.

Era structure
60-year rabjung cycles counted from the Kālacakra tantra's arrival in Tibet, 1027 CE (a fire-hare year). 2026 CE = 17th rabjung, year 40. Years are named element × animal (Fire Horse in 2026, sharing the sexagenary machinery with China, but with Losar — the new year — falling by its own computation, usually within a month of Chinese New Year).
Skipped & doubled days
A civil day is numbered by the lunar day (tithi) in force at its dawn. Because tithis run slightly shorter or longer than solar days, some numbers never touch a dawn (skipped: the month jumps from the 4th to the 6th) and some touch two (doubled: two consecutive 25ths). The date-sequence stutters and echoes by design — arithmetic honesty about the moon's uneven gait.
Machinery
Full computation (grub rtsis) descends from Kālacakra astronomy: mean and true motions in sexagesimal units, 67 lunations ≈ 65 solar months for intercalation. Svante Janson's Tibetan Calendar Mathematics (free, arXiv) gives complete algorithms — the flagged implementation source in §XIII.
Cosmological claim
Kālacakra means "wheel of time": outer time (astronomy), inner time (breath — 21,600 breaths a day, mirroring cosmic counts), and other time (the practice liberating from both). The calendar is soteriology; computing it is contemplation.

Structure
A 210-day cycle carrying ten concurrent "weeks" of lengths 1–10 (ekawara through dasawara). The era cluster — and the year of confusion

Offset eras

Attic Greece
No era at all: years named for the eponymous archon ("in the archonship of Euthydemos"), synchronized across cities only by Olympiads. Pre-Julian Rome

Every reform is preceded by a monster year that absorbs the accumulated lie.

Sogdian & Khwārizmian calendars — al-Bīrūnī's testimony

The only detailed record of two Zoroastrian-adjacent calendars that otherwise vanished — preserved by a single 11th-century polymath who thought their own dual explanation for why they disagreed with Persia was itself worth two-facing.

Primary source
Abū Rayhān al-Bīrūnī, al-Āthār al-Bāqiya ("The Remaining Vestiges," compiled c. 390 AH / 1000 CE), chapters IX–XI, in C. E. Sachau's 1879 English edition — now incorporated directly into this codex.
Structure
Both Sogdiana (Bukhara/Samarkand) and Khwārizm (the Oxus delta) ran the same 12 × 30-day month grid as the Persians, with 5 epagomenal days — but placed those five days differently, and began the year on a different day: Nausard (Sogdian) / Nausarjī (Khwārizmian), against the Persian Nowrūz.
Al-Bīrūnī's two explanations, given with equal weight
He offers both without choosing: (1) a court-etiquette account — Jam (Jamshīd) set out on campaign and returned in triumph; the Persian kings kept his departure as new-year, while Sogdiana and Khwārizm, wishing not to imitate royal practice too closely, kept his return instead; (2) an astronomical-drift account — after Zoroaster's reform the Persian priesthood re-observed the solstice, found it had shifted five days against the old calendar, and corrected the epagomenal days' placement accordingly, while Transoxiana simply never updated. A precise historical instance of exactly this codex's Isimud method, arrived at independently in 1000 CE.
Recovered festival calendar (Sogdian, selected)
Nausard 1 — Great Nauroz; Nausard 28 — Bāmūsh-Āghdām, a Bukharan Magian fire-temple assembly; Bāghakān 1 — Nīm-sardha ("mid-year"); Zhshum (year's last month), final day — a day of mourning for the dead with cut faces and offered dishes, explicitly compared by al-Bīrūnī to the Persian Farwardajān; the 8th, 15th, and 23rd of every month, called Dast, carried standing local fairs in Bukhara and Sogdiana.
Recovered festival calendar (Khwārizmian, selected)
Nausarjī 1 — new year; Īkhsharēwārī 1, "Faghrūbah" — nominally "the exitus of the Shah," the day Khwārizmian kings historically marched to winter quarters against Ghuzz Turk raids; Ūmrī 13 — Chirī-Bōj, held in the same regard as the Persian Mihragān; several day-names preserve older forms corrupted, by al-Bīrūnī's own account, through repeated colloquial use — language visibly eroding under a live oral calendar in real time, recorded by a contemporary.
Why this matters here
Sogdian and Khwārizmian are functionally extinct as living calendars; without al-Bīrūnī there would be no month-by-month festival record at all. He is, for this pair of systems, the entire outward face and the last surviving inward witness at once — the two-faced method is not optional here, it is all that remains.

IV · Beneath the Year

Cycles smaller than the year

The meaning layer does not begin at the year. Below it runs a second stratum — weeks, market-rounds, day-qualities, and partitions of the day itself — where most of a life's actual scheduling has always happened. The week is the most ideological object in this document: it corresponds to no astronomical period at all, and it has never been broken.

The week and the planets

The 7-day week fuses two independent inventions: the Jewish Sabbath cycle (a pure count, indifferent to sun and moon) and the Hellenistic planetary week. In the latter, each of the 24 hours is ruled by a planet in Chaldean order (Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon, repeating); the ruler of each day's first hour names the day — and stepping 24 hours through the sequence yields exactly the day-order Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn. English preserves the system through Norse translation (Tiw/Mars, Woden/Mercury, Thor/Jupiter, Frigg/Venus); Romance languages keep the planets raw (martedì, mercredi); Portuguese abolished them for numbered feiras. Constantine made dies Solis the civil rest day in 321 CE — the moment the two weeks fused. Every subsequent attempt to break the week (the French décade, the Soviet nepreryvka) failed within 15 years. Nothing enforces the week except everyone.

Market weeks and concurrent rounds

Much of the world kept shorter weeks tuned to trade, not planets: the Roman 8-day nundinae (fasti inscribed with letters A–H; it ran beside the 7-day week for centuries — lcm 56 days — until the seven won); the Igbo 4-day week (Eke, Orie, Afọ, Nkwọ — market days that still name people and places) and its 8-day izu ukwu; the Yoruba 4-day round; the Akan 6-day cycle meshing with the 7-day week into the 42-day adaduanan that schedules the Adae festivals; the Javanese 5-day pasaran (Legi, Pahing, Pon, Wage, Kliwon) meshing with the 7-day week into the 35-day wetonan — a person's weton, their birth-intersection, is a lifelong divinatory coordinate. Bali's Pawukon (§III) is this principle run to ten concurrent gears.

Decans and pentads

Egypt divided the year into 36 decans — ten-day "weeks," each marked by a star-group whose dawn rising took over the count; the decans rising through the night divided darkness into 12, which is where the 12-hour night, and thus the 24-hour day, comes from. The hours on every clock are the fossil of a ten-day Egyptian week. China divided the tropical year into 24 solar terms and further into 72 hou — five-day pentads, each named for a micro-seasonal event ("east wind melts the ice"; "hawks are transformed into doves"): a phenological calendar inside the astronomical one.

Small multi-year rounds

The shmita: every 7th Hebrew year (AM divisible by 7) the land rests and debts release — AM 5782 (2021–22) was shmita; 5789 (2028–29) is next. The 50th-year jubilee (yovel) stands uncounted since antiquity: a cycle held in the text but not in time. The indiction: a 15-year Roman tax-assessment round (from 312 CE) with no astronomical content whatsoever, which outlived the empire by a millennium in papal and Byzantine dating — and became one of Scaliger's three gears for the Julian Period (§II). Rome's lustrum (5-year census purification) and the Olympiad (4 years) complete the family. The engine computes indiction and flags shmita for any date.

Day-quality systems

Calendars that grade days rather than count them: the Egyptian lucky/unlucky day lists (the Cairo Calendar marks each day's thirds favorable or hostile with mythological warrants — days the Eye of Sekhmet rages, days Horus was healed); the Roman fasti letters (F lawful for business, N nefastus, C for assemblies, NP for festivals) — a legal machine layered on the month, its days counted backward and inclusively from Kalends, Nones, and Ides; the Coligny plaque's MAT/ANM gradings; Tzolk'in and weton divination; the Chinese almanac (tongshu) day-grades still consulted for weddings, contracts, and burials. This is the meaning layer at daily resolution.

Partitions of the day

Temporal (unequal) hours — daylight ÷ 12, night ÷ 12 — ruled antiquity and survive in two living systems: halakhic hours (shaʿot zemaniyot) governing Jewish prayer windows, and the Islamic five prayers (Fajr, Dhuhr, ʿAsr, Maghrib, ʿIshāʾ), a partition of the day by pure solar geometry — a sundial liturgy now computed by formula. Christian monasticism fixed the canonical hours (Matins, Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline); None, the ninth hour, drifted earlier until it named noon itself. China ran 12 double-hours (shíchen) named by the earthly branches, the Rat hour spanning midnight; Japan kept temporal hours with seasonally re-geared clocks (wadokei) until 1873, bells counting 9 down to 4. The Hebrew ḥelek (1/1080 hour, §III) and the Hindu ladder (60 ghaṭikā × 60 pala × 60 vipala — 24 minutes to the ghaṭikā) complete the stratum: below the day, Babylon's base-60 owns nearly everything.

VI · Temporal Fault Lines

Where multiple systems mark a transition at once

The framework's most charged objects: moments where independent counting systems cluster their zeros, breaks, or completions.

c. 3200–3100 BCE

The great cluster of beginnings

Within roughly one century: writing appears at Uruk (~3200); Egypt unifies under Narmer (~3100); the Mayan Long Count epoch (3114); the Kali Yuga epoch (3102); megalithic Newgrange (~3200) and the first phase of Stonehenge (~3000); Cycladic and early Norte Chico florescence. Two unrelated civilizations independently placed creation itself in this band — and it is, archaeologically, the band where history (writing, states, monumental time-keeping) actually begins. The 5.9-kiloyear climate event (aridification, ~3900 BCE onward) sits just behind it.

c. 2350–2150 BCE

The 4.2-kiloyear event

A megadrought marks the fall of the Akkadian Empire, the Egyptian Old Kingdom's collapse into the First Intermediate Period, and crises from the Indus to China — the geological Meghalayan Age boundary is defined by it. The first empirically-dated "end of an age" shared across systems that never knew each other.

c. 1200–1150 BCE

Late Bronze Age collapse

Within ~50 years: Mycenae, Hattusa, Ugarit, and Alashiya burn; Egypt repels the Sea Peoples but declines; writing systems (Linear B) die; the eastern Mediterranean enters a dark age. A systems-collapse so total that Greek memory converted it into myth (the Trojan War, the Heroic Age's end) — an era that survived only as legend inside the calendar of its successors.

c. 800–200 BCE

The Axial Age

Jaspers' observation: Buddha, Mahāvīra, Confucius, Laozi, Zoroaster (by late dating), the Hebrew prophets, and the Greek philosophers cluster in one band — the period when most of the eschatologies in section IV were drafted. The age when time itself became a moral object.

539 BCE · 312 BCE

Babylon's fall and the invention of the era

Cyrus takes Babylon (539) — end of the Neo-Babylonian order, return from Exile, Second Temple horizon; Daniel's visions are set here. Then the Seleucid Era (312/311) makes the count itself immortal: the first year-count that outlives its founder. Every subsequent era — Christian, Islamic, Republican — descends from this move.

622 CE

The Hijra

One event spawns two calendars — lunar Hijrī and Solar Hijrī — that share a zero and diverge ~11 days a year, now ~43 years apart (AH 1447 vs. SH 1405 in 2026). The cleanest natural experiment in what a calendar's structure, independent of its epoch, does to meaning.

1582–1926 CE

The Gregorian cascade

A 344-year fault line moving across the planet: ten days deleted in Rome (1582), eleven in London (1752), thirteen in Moscow (1918) and Istanbul (1926). During the overlap, Europe ran on two dates at once — letters were double-dated O.S./N.S.; Shakespeare and Cervantes "died on the same date" (23 April 1616) ten real days apart.

1792 · 1844

Revolutionary zeros

France restarts the count at the Republic (1792). Then 1844: the Báb's declaration founds the Badíʿ calendar in Persia while, the same year, the Millerite Great Disappointment (22 Oct 1844) breaks and re-forms American apocalypticism (Adventism is its child) — and Morse's telegraph ("What hath God wrought", 24 May 1844) begins the annihilation of distance that will force standardized time zones (1884 Prime Meridian Conference) onto the world.

1945–1970 CE

Radiocarbon's "present" is frozen at 1950 because atmospheric bomb tests destroyed the baseline — the atomic age broke dating itself. Layer 2 — where the year began

Before standardization, "the year" changed number on different days in different cities — a second map hiding under the first. Styles: 1 January (Circumcision style, Roman civil); 1 March (Venice, until 1797); 25 March (Annunciation / Lady Day — England to 1751; Florence and Pisa to 1749, with Pisa numbered one year ahead of Florence); Easter (France until 1564 — a mobile New Year, so some years contained two Aprils); 1 September (Byzantine); 25 December (Christmas style, medieval Germany, England before the 12th c.). A traveler from Venice to Pisa to Paris in a single (Julian) March could pass through three different year-numbers without the date changing.

Layer 3 — the timezone stratum

The 1884 International Meridian Conference fixed Greenwich as zero and implied the antimeridian date line — the one line on Earth where the calendar day itself is torn. The line is set by no treaty and moves by national choice: Samoa deleted 30 December 2011 to jump west across it; Kiribati moved it ~2,000 km east in 1995 so its whole territory (and the millennium's "first sunrise") shared one date; Alaska crossed it by purchase in 1867. Offsets encode politics, not geography: Nepal at +5:45, India's single +5:30, China's one zone spanning what is solar-time five, Spain running Central European Time since 1940. The authoritative machine-readable record is the IANA tz database — every zone a file of historical transitions, effectively the legal history of local time on Earth; pair it with this file's JDN spine and the map layer is complete.

Implementation notes for the app

  • Timelines: render one horizontal JDN axis; draw each system as a stratum whose tick marks are its own new years, cycle stations, and era boundaries. Fault lines become vertical seams crossing all strata.
  • Maps: geocode each system's origin (Uruk, Ujjain, Jerusalem, Mecca, the Petén, Rome, Alexandria, Paris, Shiraz/Tehran, Greenwich) and animate adoption fronts (the Gregorian cascade of 1582–1926 is a ready-made animated map).
  • The meaning readout: for a given JDN, emit per system: (a) its coordinates, (b) distance from epoch, (c) position in its largest cycle, (d) proximity to its nearest eschatological or cycle station, (e) whether the moment is before its count began — the "before-the-count" state is data, not absence.
  • Precision honesty: tag every output with its confidence class — exact (arithmetic calendars), ±1–2 d (observational lunar), ±years (regnal/archaeological), contested (correlation constants, ancient chronologies). The uncertainty is part of the content.
